ON STATISTICS AND STD DEV

19th April 2026

Something that has always helped me understand mathematical jargon and formula better is having a parellel to some physical property and to the transformations that may be made with that property. Like back in grade school where I learned about addition and basic multiplication by imagining groups of apples and other fruits. More than once I recall learning about an algorithm and how to implement it while missing an understanding of why it works until I learned the physical parellels of its components.

The standard deviation is a property that I had never been given a comfortable parellel for. So I have always thought of it as a kind of "base" property in itself, which is good enough. After all, I have no trouble calculating and using the standard deviation. It has as a result however, always felt like my understanding of it was incomplete.

I have been reading through 'The Scientist and Engineer's Guide to Digital Signal Processing' by Steven W. Smith (DSPGuide) as it came highly recommended to me by some engineer's much smarter than myself whenever I have asked them for recommendations. It has a chapter introducing statistics and probability which of course includes a section dedicated to explaining the mean and standard deviation. In that section, I found the following:

"The standard deviation is similar to the average deviation, except the averaging is done with power instead of amplitude. This is achieved by squaring each of the deviations before taking the average (remember, power is proportional to voltage squared)."

...Huh.

I chatted about it with a few more engineers, did some further reading, and scribbled a few pages of math into my notebook. That helped me pick up a few more tidbits relating standard deviation along with other statistical properties and formula to physical parellels like voltage, power, etc. Up until now, I have only been able to understand these formula and properties from statistics and probability as abstract notions which I used to parametrically describe sequences of numbers. Now I have a clearer understanding of what makes those properties and formula so helpful for making those descriptions.

This may have all been very obvious for Electrical Engineers who are no stranger to calculations with voltage, resistance, and power. But, for the computer scientist like myself whose entire understanding of these electrical concepts is self taught and rather lacking (but rapidly improving!), it is new frame from which to understand statistics. Or at least it is for myself.
